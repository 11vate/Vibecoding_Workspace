# Tension Curves

## Purpose

Design and management of tension curves, creating engaging experiences through well-designed tension and release cycles.

**Tension curves create engagement through challenge and relief.**

---

## Tension Curve Structure

### Curve Phases

**Rising Tension**:
- Challenge increases
- Stakes rise
- Uncertainty grows
- Pressure builds

**Peak Tension**:
- Maximum challenge
- Highest stakes
- Greatest uncertainty
- Peak pressure

**Release**:
- Challenge resolved
- Stakes resolved
- Uncertainty resolved
- Pressure released

**Recovery**:
- Return to baseline
- Rest period
- Preparation
- Reflection

---

## Tension Curve Types

### Steady Rise

**Pattern**: Gradual tension increase to peak.

**Use Case**: Building to climax.

**Characteristics**:
- Gradual increase
- Steady build
- Clear peak
- Satisfying release

**Why**: Steady rise creates anticipation.

---

### Peaks and Valleys

**Pattern**: Alternating tension peaks and valleys.

**Use Case**: Maintaining engagement.

**Characteristics**:
- Multiple peaks
- Recovery valleys
- Varied intensity
- Sustained engagement

**Why**: Peaks and valleys prevent monotony.

---

### Sudden Spikes

**Pattern**: Quick tension spikes.

**Use Case**: Surprise, excitement.

**Characteristics**:
- Quick rise
- High peak
- Fast release
- Surprise element

**Why**: Sudden spikes create excitement.

---

## Tension Design

### Tension Sources

**Sources**:
- **Challenge** - Difficulty, skill required
- **Risk** - Potential loss, consequences
- **Time** - Time pressure, deadlines
- **Uncertainty** - Unknown outcomes, ambiguity

**Why**: Multiple sources create varied tension.

---

### Tension Intensity

**Intensity Levels**:
- **Low** - Mild challenge, low stakes
- **Medium** - Moderate challenge, medium stakes
- **High** - Significant challenge, high stakes
- **Extreme** - Maximum challenge, critical stakes

**Why**: Varied intensity creates engagement.

---

### Tension Duration

**Duration Types**:
- **Brief** - Quick tension, fast release
- **Medium** - Moderate duration, satisfying release
- **Extended** - Long tension, delayed release
- **Sustained** - Continuous tension, periodic release

**Why**: Varied duration prevents fatigue.

---

## Release Design

### Release Types

**Types**:
- **Resolution** - Problem solved, challenge overcome
- **Relief** - Pressure removed, stress reduced
- **Reward** - Achievement, recognition, reward
- **Completion** - Goal achieved, task completed

**Why**: Varied releases create satisfaction.

---

### Release Timing

**Timing Principles**:
- **Before Overwhelm** - Release before too much
- **After Investment** - Release after effort
- **At Peak** - Release at tension peak
- **Periodic** - Regular releases

**Why**: Proper timing maximizes satisfaction.

---

## Recovery Design

### Recovery Purpose

**Purpose**: Allow emotional and mental recovery.

**Recovery Elements**:
- Lower intensity periods
- Rest opportunities
- Reflection time
- Preparation time

**Why**: Recovery prevents burnout.

---

### Recovery Timing

**When to Provide**:
- After high tension
- After failures
- Between challenges
- At natural breaks

**Why**: Timely recovery maintains engagement.

## How Cursor Uses Tension Curves

### When Designing Experiences

Cursor must:
1. **Design tension curves** - Rising, peak, release, recovery
2. **Vary tension sources** - Challenge, risk, time, uncertainty
3. **Control tension intensity** - Low to extreme
4. **Time releases** - Before overwhelm, after investment
5. **Enable recovery** - After tension, between challenges

**Never design experiences without tension curves.**

---

### When Evaluating Designs

Cursor must ask:
- Are tension curves designed?
- Is tension varied?
- Are releases timed well?
- Is recovery provided?

**Never accept designs without tension curves.**
